California's New E-Waste Laws: What Every OC Business Must Know in 2025

December 14, 2024|8 min read|By OC Electronic Recycling Team
Compliance Security Recycling

What's Changed in 2025

California has long been at the forefront of electronic waste regulation, and 2025 brings significant updates that Orange County businesses cannot afford to ignore. The California Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC) has expanded its oversight of covered electronic devices, and penalties for non-compliance have increased substantially.

Under the Electronic Waste Recycling Act (SB 20/50), most businesses in California are already required to properly dispose of covered electronic devices through certified recyclers. But recent amendments extend these requirements to a broader category of equipment — including networking gear, enterprise storage, and certain peripheral devices previously excluded.

Who Is Affected

If your business in Orange County generates more than 50 pounds of electronic waste per year — which includes any company with more than a handful of employees — you are subject to California's e-waste disposal regulations. This covers:

  • Healthcare providers (with additional HIPAA data obligations)
  • Financial services firms (with GLBA data protection requirements)
  • Educational institutions (with FERPA considerations)
  • Any business with regular IT equipment turnover cycles

Data Destruction Is Non-Negotiable

California's SB 1386 and AB 1950 impose strict requirements on the protection of personal information — including data stored on disposed devices. Simply recycling a hard drive without verifying data destruction is not sufficient under California law.

The required standard for most businesses is NIST Special Publication 800-88, which outlines three levels of data sanitization: Clear, Purge, and Destroy. Physical shredding satisfies the Destroy level and is the most defensible approach for high-security environments.

The Cost of Non-Compliance

Fines for improper e-waste disposal in California can reach $70,000 per day for ongoing violations. Data breach penalties under SB 1386 can reach $2,500 per affected record — a single improperly disposed drive can represent enormous liability if it contains customer or employee data.

How to Stay Compliant

Compliance is straightforward when you work with a certified recycler. Here's what a compliant process looks like:

  • Use a California DTSC authorized collector or recycler
  • Ensure all data-bearing devices receive data destruction certificates
  • Maintain records of all disposal activities for a minimum of 3 years
  • Request proof of downstream compliance from your recycler

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my small business need to follow e-waste regulations?+

Yes. California's e-waste regulations apply to all businesses that generate covered electronic waste, regardless of size. Even a 5-person office turning over a handful of laptops per year is subject to requirements under the Electronic Waste Recycling Act.

Is deleting files enough before recycling a computer?+

No. Deleting files or formatting a drive does not constitute data destruction under NIST 800-88 or California law. Data can be recovered from formatted drives using widely available tools. Proper data destruction requires certified wiping software or physical destruction.

How do I get a certificate of data destruction?+

When you use OC Electronic Recycling for data destruction, we issue a certificate for each device within 48 hours of processing. The certificate includes the device serial number, destruction method, technician ID, and timestamp.

What if I donate old computers instead of recycling?+

Donating computers is acceptable under California law, but you are still responsible for ensuring data is properly wiped before donation. We can wipe drives before donating — call us at (949) 345-0285.
